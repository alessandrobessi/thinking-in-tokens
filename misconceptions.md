# Misconception Graph

A running index of the misconception graph described in blueprint.md. Each
row mirrors a "Common Misconceptions" block from a chapter — this table
exists so a later chapter never re-introduces the same misconception under a
different analogy without noticing. The `ID` column is the stable key
referenced by `concept-graph.yaml`'s `misconception_ids`.

| ID | Concept | Misconception | Correct Intuition | Analogy | Chapter |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `ai-consciousness-overnight` | Modern AI's origin | AI became conscious / started truly understanding overnight. | Public *access* crossed a threshold after years of steady, gradual improvement. | A pot of water doesn't decide to boil — it crosses 100°C after a steady rise. | Ch. 1 |
| `ai-vs-bigger-chatbot` | Modern AI vs. chatbots | This is just a bigger version of old scripted chatbots. | The shift is from matching input to a pre-written script, to generating plausible text word by word. | A phrasebook vs. a fluent speaker. | Ch. 1 |
| `computation-implies-understanding` | Computation | A computer that processes information understands what it means. | Computation requires only mechanical rule-following, not comprehension. | A vending machine processes coins without knowing what money is. | Ch. 2 |
| `more-data-equals-more-information` | Information | More information means more data / a longer message. | Information measures reduction of uncertainty, not length. | A 100-page forecast saying "same as always" tells you almost nothing. | Ch. 2 |
| `model-reads-letter-by-letter` | Tokenization | The model reads text one letter at a time. | The model reads a sequence of tokens, mostly whole words or common fragments. | A fluent reader recognizes "the" instantly, not letter by letter. | Ch. 3 |
| `token-always-one-word` | Tokens | A token is always exactly one word. | A token is whatever chunk got assigned a single ID — word, fragment, or less. | Postal abbreviations: common words get short codes, rare ones spelled out. | Ch. 3 |
| `compression-loses-meaning` | Compression | Compressing language loses quality/meaning, like a blurry photo. | Compression removes what's predictable; what remains is the informative core. | Telegram-speak drops "I will be" but keeps "Tuesday." | Ch. 4 |
| `context-is-just-topic` | Context | Context just means the general topic being discussed. | Context is the exact surrounding token sequence used to resolve ambiguity. | Knowing the topic is a river; knowing the prior sentence tells you which "bank" is meant. | Ch. 4 |
| `embeddings-store-definitions` | Embeddings | The model stores dictionary definitions. | The model learns locations in a geometric space. | Cities on a map. | Ch. 5 |
| `similarity-means-synonym` | Similarity | Similarity just means "these words are synonyms." | Closeness reflects similarity of use/context, a broader relationship than synonymy. | Rival neighboring capitals sit close on a culture/economy map despite being opposites. | Ch. 5 |
| `embedding-is-permanent-meaning` | Embeddings (static vs. contextual) | An embedding is a word's one true, permanent representation of meaning. | This chapter's embedding is a token's starting location; attention (Ch. 11) revises it per-sentence into a contextual representation. | A home address is fixed; where the person actually is right now changes throughout the day. | Ch. 5 |
| `model-plans-whole-sentence` | Prediction | The model plans out the entire sentence in advance, then writes it down. | Generation is autoregressive: each token is predicted one at a time, using only what's been generated so far. | A jazz musician improvises note by note without a pre-written solo. | Ch. 6 |
| `prediction-is-lookup` | Prediction | Predicting the next token means looking up the answer in a giant table of memorized sentences. | The model computes a fresh probability distribution from context every time; it isn't matching a stored table. | A forecaster computes a forecast from current conditions, not a logbook of identical past days. | Ch. 6 |
| `bigger-counting-table-works` | N-gram models | A big enough counting table would eventually solve prediction — just a matter of more data. | The space of possible sequences grows explosively with length; no realistic amount of counting closes the gap. | No cookbook can contain the exact recipe for every ingredient combination in your kitchen. | Ch. 7 |
| `ai-is-bigger-counting-table` | N-gram models vs. neural networks | Modern AI is just a much bigger version of the counting-table approach. | The mechanism is different in kind: a neural network learns a generalizable function; a counting table only reports exact historical frequencies. | A phrasebook ten times longer is still just a phrasebook. | Ch. 7 |
| `nn-simulates-brain` | Neural networks | A neural network is basically a simulation of a human brain. | Units are only loosely, historically inspired by biological neurons; there's no claim of biological accuracy. | A Boeing 747 is inspired by bird wings but doesn't simulate a bird. | Ch. 8 |
| `more-parameters-more-understanding` | Neural networks / parameters | More parameters automatically means the network understands more, in a simple linear way. | Parameter count is one ingredient among several (data, training balance) — Chapter 10 covers how they interact. | A bigger engine doesn't make a car faster if the rest of the car isn't built to use that power. | Ch. 8 |
| `training-is-memorization` | Training / learning | Training makes the model memorize its training examples like a stored database. | Training rewards parameters that generalize across enormous, varied examples; memorization is a special-case failure mode, not the norm. | A student doing thousands of practice problems learns the method, not a lookup table of those exact problems. | Ch. 9 |
| `lower-loss-is-more-intelligence` | Loss | A lower loss always means the model has gotten more generally "intelligent." | Loss measures fit to the specific training objective (next-token prediction), not general intelligence. | A high score on one exam is real but isn't a complete measure of a student's intelligence. | Ch. 9 |
| `scaling-laws-unlimited` | Scaling laws | Scaling laws mean you can scale forever with no real limit. | Returns diminish, and practical ceilings exist in data, compute cost, and energy. | The tenth month of marathon training improves your time far less than the first month did. | Ch. 10 |
| `bigger-model-always-better` | Scaling laws | A bigger model is automatically the better choice for any given use case. | Scaling laws predict lower loss on the training objective, not the right cost/speed/capability tradeoff for a specific task. | A commercial airliner isn't the better choice for a quick hop between two nearby small towns. | Ch. 10 |
| `attention-reads-sequentially` | Attention | Attention means the model reads one word at a time, left to right, like a person reading a sentence. | Attention weighs every token against every other token simultaneously — a parallel weighing, not a sequential scan. | A conductor attends to the whole ensemble at once, not one instrument at a time in sequence. | Ch. 11 |
| `positional-encoding-teaches-grammar` | Positional encoding | Positional encoding gives the model an understanding of grammar and word order, like a person learning syntax. | It's just a numerical position tag; any grammatical pattern built from it has to be learned during training, like everything else. | Numbering a shuffled manuscript's pages tells you the order, not what the story means. | Ch. 11 |
| `attention-sees-future-tokens` | Attention / causal masking | Since attention lets a model weigh every token's relevance, it can look at the entire sentence, including words that come later — the same way a human reading the whole sentence at once can. | Attention is deliberately restricted, by the causal mask, to the current token and everything before it — never anything after — because next-token prediction requires it. | A detective solving a case in real time can only use clues discovered so far, not clues that will turn up tomorrow. | Ch. 11 |
| `every-transformer-layer-same` | Transformer blocks | Every layer in a transformer does the same thing — identical, repeated computation. | Each block has its own independently learned parameters, and blocks tend to specialize in different kinds of patterns as a byproduct of training. | Twenty-four editors reading the same manuscript don't make identical changes, even though they're all "editing." | Ch. 12 |
| `more-layers-just-slower` | Transformer blocks | Adding more layers just makes the same computation take longer, without changing what the model can do. | More layers give qualitatively more rounds of refinement, not just more time spent on one computation. | Twenty-four rounds of editing produce a qualitatively more polished manuscript than one editor working twenty-four times as long alone. | Ch. 12 |
| `chatgpt-is-base-model-with-interface` | Base vs. chat model | ChatGPT is just a base model like GPT-3, exposed directly through a chat interface. | Substantial additional training (fine-tuning, human feedback) happens after pretraining specifically to shape helpful, conversational behavior. | A brilliant graduate still needs coaching before becoming a good teacher. | Ch. 13 |
| `transformer-paper-alone-explains-quality` | Architecture vs. alignment | The 2017 transformer paper alone explains why chatbots suddenly got good. | Architecture, data, and compute produced a powerful base model; the specific helpful, conversational behavior required additional fine-tuning and human-feedback training on top. | A powerful engine doesn't make a car street-legal or comfortable to drive on its own. | Ch. 13 |
| `model-learns-during-conversation` | Inference | The model is still learning or improving while you chat with it. | Inference uses frozen, already-trained parameters; nothing about a conversation changes the model's underlying trained knowledge. | A doctor taking your case history during an exam isn't relearning medicine. | Ch. 14 |
| `same-prompt-same-answer-always` | Sampling | Since it's the same model and the same prompt, it should give the exact same answer every time. | Sampling deliberately introduces controlled randomness by design, unless explicitly configured otherwise. | Three equally excellent writers finishing the same sentence naturally produce three different, reasonable endings. | Ch. 14 |
| `hallucination-is-rare-bug` | Hallucination | Hallucination is a rare bug that will simply get fixed as models improve. | It's a structural tension between "always produce a fluent, confident continuation" and "only state true things," not a simple coding error awaiting a patch. | A student trained to always sound confident will keep bluffing convincingly even after more coaching, unless specifically retrained not to. | Ch. 15 |
| `model-knows-its-making-things-up` | Hallucination | The model knows it's making something up and is choosing to deceive. | There's no separate module tracking "what's actually true" apart from "what token comes next" — any internal signal correlated with uncertainty isn't reliably surfaced as calibrated confidence in normal generation. | A fluent public speaker sounds equally confident reciting a verified fact or a half-remembered detail. | Ch. 15 |
| `context-window-is-trained-knowledge` | Context window | The context window is the same thing as the model's trained knowledge or long-term memory. | Trained knowledge persists across every conversation; the context window is a separate, temporary, per-conversation input buffer. | A doctor's medical training doesn't reset between patients, but their notes on today's patient are specific to this one visit. | Ch. 16 |
| `model-remembers-fuzzily-like-person` | Memory | A model remembers earlier parts of a long conversation the way a person does, just imperfectly. | Information is either still present as tokens in the current input, or entirely absent — there's no fuzzy, partial, human-style memory in between. | A photocopier either has a page in the tray or it doesn't. | Ch. 16 |
| `retrieval-is-smarter-keyword-search` | Retrieval | Retrieval works by searching for matching keywords, just with some extra intelligence layered on. | Semantic retrieval finds meaning-level matches even with zero shared words, comparing locations in embedding space, not overlapping vocabulary. | A librarian who organizes by meaning finds you the right book by what it's about, not by shared title words. | Ch. 17 |
| `vector-db-is-regular-db-with-search` | Vector databases | A vector database is just a regular database with a search feature added. | Its core operation — approximate nearest-neighbor search over high-dimensional points — is fundamentally different from what conventional databases optimize for. | A library organized by meaning and one organized alphabetically are solving different problems, even though both are "libraries." | Ch. 17 |
| `rag-means-retrained-on-documents` | RAG | RAG means the model has been retrained on the retrieved documents. | No training or parameter update happens — it's entirely a context-window technique performed fresh at each query. | Handing someone a reference sheet during a test doesn't require re-teaching them the whole subject. | Ch. 18 |
| `rag-eliminates-hallucination-entirely` | RAG | RAG eliminates hallucination entirely, since the model now has real sources to work from. | The model can still misread or contradict retrieved text, or hallucinate when retrieval itself fails to find the relevant passage — risk is reduced, not removed. | An open-book exam reduces guessing, but a student can still misread the reference page. | Ch. 18 |
| `finetuning-pretraining-fundamentally-different` | Fine-tuning | Fine-tuning and pretraining are fundamentally different kinds of processes. | Fine-tuning reuses the identical predict/measure-error/adjust loop from Chapter 9 — only the dataset and goal change. | A medical residency uses the same underlying learning process as medical school, just applied to a narrower set of cases. | Ch. 19 |
| `aligned-model-genuinely-understands-values` | Alignment | An aligned model has been made to genuinely understand and agree with human values. | Alignment adjusts which outputs a model tends to produce based on favorably-rated patterns — a behavioral adjustment, not demonstrated comprehension or agreement. | A well-trained customer service employee can reliably follow a company's values without that proving they personally hold those values. | Ch. 19 |
| `quantization-makes-model-dumber` | Quantization | Quantization makes a model noticeably dumber, in a way any user would clearly notice. | At reasonable levels, accuracy loss is typically small enough to be barely noticeable, though it's a genuine tradeoff and very aggressive quantization can degrade quality. | A well-made JPEG looks essentially identical to its RAW source, though compressing far more aggressively eventually shows. | Ch. 20 |
| `efficient-model-is-different-smaller-model` | Efficient inference | An efficient or quantized version of a model is a smaller, differently-trained model. | The exact same trained model's weights are represented more coarsely or served differently — not a genuinely smaller or differently structured model trained from scratch. | A compressed photo and its RAW original show the same scene; compressing the file didn't send a different photographer to reshoot it smaller. | Ch. 20 |
| `tool-execution-happens-inside-model` | Tool calling | When a model "uses a tool," it's directly running code or reaching onto the internet itself. | The model only produces a structured text request; a separate system outside the model parses and executes it. | The analyst doesn't walk onto the trading floor herself — she sends a request and waits for someone else to act on it. | Ch. 21 |
| `model-calls-any-tool-it-imagines` | Tool calling | Tool calling means the model can use whatever tool it decides it needs, on its own initiative. | The model can only request tools explicitly described to it in advance in a fixed tool schema — it cannot invent access to a capability nobody exposed to it. | The analyst's colleague can look up a stock price because that request line exists — he can't approve a wire transfer just because she phrases a request for one. | Ch. 21 |
| `mcp-is-new-ai-capability` | Model Context Protocol (MCP) | MCP is a fundamentally smarter or more capable kind of AI, not just a connection standard. | MCP standardizes how tools/data sources describe themselves and connect to model-serving applications — it adds no new reasoning ability. | A standardized electrical outlet doesn't make an appliance more powerful — it just lets any compliant appliance plug into any compliant socket without a custom adapter. | Ch. 21 |
| `agent-is-different-model` | AI agents | An agent is a fundamentally different, more advanced kind of AI model. | An agent uses the exact same underlying model and generation/tool-calling mechanism — "agent" describes the surrounding loop, not a new model or architecture. | The same capable employee could be asked one question and stop, or given a goal and left to work unsupervised — the difference is the situation, not the person. | Ch. 22 |
| `agent-has-genuine-autonomy` | AI agents | An agent has genuine autonomy or goals of its own, independent of what it was asked to do. | At each step the model is still predicting the most useful next output given its current context, including the original task — it has no persistent desires beyond that. | The employee's initiative is bounded by the manager's actual request, not a personal agenda she's pursuing. | Ch. 22 |
| `agent-looping-means-more-accurate` | AI agents | Because an agent checks its own work across multiple steps, it's reliably more accurate than a single response. | Each step is generated the same fallible way as any other output; a wrong turn can compound across steps just as easily as it can get corrected. | Giving the employee more unsupervised time can produce a better report — or send her further down the wrong path, unless someone checks in. | Ch. 22 |
| `reasoning-model-thinks-like-human` | Reasoning models | A reasoning model is deliberately thinking behind the scenes, the way a person reflects before speaking. | Every reasoning token is generated by the same next-token prediction mechanism as any other token — there's no separate hidden cognitive process. | The second student isn't running a fundamentally different kind of brain — she's using the same mind to write out the steps, and the writing itself improves the outcome. | Ch. 23 |
| `longer-reasoning-always-more-correct` | Reasoning models | Longer reasoning always means a more correct answer. | Each reasoning step is still produced by the same fallible generation process and can itself state a wrong step confidently; excessive reasoning on an easy problem can introduce unnecessary uncertainty. | A student who overthinks a trivially simple question can talk themselves into the wrong answer. | Ch. 23 |
| `reasoning-trace-is-faithful-readout` | Reasoning models | The reasoning a model prints out is a transparent, fully accurate window into its actual internal computation. | Printed reasoning genuinely shapes the final answer by becoming context, but that's different from being a guaranteed, faithful transcript of the model's actual internal computation. | A student's scratch paper shapes what she writes next and is worth reading — but it's her account of her thinking, not a neural readout. | Ch. 23 |
| `multimodal-is-two-models-glued` | Multimodality | A multimodal model is really two separate models — one for vision, one for language — glued together. | Each modality needs its own encoder for initial conversion, but once converted, one shared transformer processes every modality's embeddings together, not two separate systems exchanging summaries. | The local doesn't run a separate photo brain and language brain — he converts what he's shown into one shared understanding. | Ch. 24 |
| `model-sees-like-human` | Multimodality | A model that processes images "sees" the way a human visually perceives the world. | The model converts pixel patches into embeddings using the same learned, pattern-based mapping used for text, with no visual experience involved. | Recognizing a street in a photo isn't the same kind of event as standing on that street and perceiving it directly. | Ch. 24 |
| `new-modality-automatic` | Multimodality | Since a model can already process images, it can handle any new kind of input automatically, with no additional training. | Supporting a new modality requires training that modality's own encoder to land in the shared representation space — nothing extends there for free. | Recognizing a friend's face doesn't automatically teach you to recognize their voice on the phone. | Ch. 24 |
| `moe-experts-are-labeled-topics` | Mixture of Experts | An MoE model has one expert per topic — a "math expert," a "history expert" — that a person could identify and label. | Routing is learned automatically during training; individual experts don't cleanly correspond to interpretable human categories. | The law firm's intake coordinator doesn't route by a clean textbook category system — real cases blend specialties. | Ch. 25 |
| `moe-cheap-as-dense-model` | Mixture of Experts | Since only a couple of experts are used per token, an MoE model is just as cheap to run as a small dense model of that active size. | Every expert's parameters still have to be stored regardless of activation; MoE reduces compute cost, not memory footprint. | The hospital still pays to keep every specialist on staff, even on a day when only two see patients. | Ch. 25 |
| `moe-is-separate-models` | Mixture of Experts | Mixture of Experts means the model is literally made of several completely separate smaller models. | The router and every expert are trained jointly inside one architecture and one training run, sharing the rest of the transformer — not independently trained models glued together. | The law firm's partners are colleagues who trained together at the same firm, not solo practitioners sharing a building. | Ch. 25 |
| `benchmark-score-means-better` | Evaluation | A high benchmark score means a model is definitely better at real-world use of that skill. | Contamination and Goodhart's-law-style overfitting can inflate a score without any corresponding real-world capability improvement. | The hiring manager's quiz score stopped meaning "good programmer" once candidates started optimizing for the quiz itself. | Ch. 26 |
| `llm-judge-as-trustworthy-as-human` | Evaluation | Using an LLM to judge another LLM's outputs is just as trustworthy as having a human expert review it directly. | A judge model can hallucinate a confident justification for a wrong verdict and can be gamed by outputs styled to appeal to its known preferences. | A trusted colleague's quick read is useful and often right, but it isn't the same guarantee as a full independent audit. | Ch. 26 |
| `higher-overall-score-strictly-better` | Evaluation | If a new model version scores higher on the overall benchmark, it's strictly better at everything the old model was good at. | An aggregate score averages across many sub-skills; a genuine regression on one capability can be masked by improvement on others. | A student's overall exam average can rise even while their grasp of one specific topic quietly gets worse. | Ch. 26 |
| `evaluation-passed-means-no-monitoring` | Observability | If a system passed its evaluation suite before launch, it's been fully verified and doesn't need further monitoring. | Offline evaluation only tests a fixed, limited set of cases; real production traffic routinely surfaces failure patterns no evaluation set anticipated. | The pre-flight checklist verifies the aircraft is fit to fly — it says nothing about the weather it will actually fly through six hours later. | Ch. 27 |
| `ai-engineering-means-training-models` | AI engineering | AI engineering means training or fine-tuning models. | Most AI engineering work never trains a foundation model from scratch — the actual work is building and operating the surrounding system around an existing pretrained model. | Building and maintaining an airline's flight operations is a different job from designing the aircraft's engine. | Ch. 27 |
| `single-error-means-system-broken` | Observability | A single error or customer complaint means the whole system is broken and needs to be pulled. | Aggregated metrics and tracing across many requests distinguish an isolated incident from a systemic pattern — something a single anecdote can't do alone. | One passenger reporting a bumpy patch of air isn't the same signal as instruments showing a sustained loss of altitude. | Ch. 27 |
| `injection-is-like-sql-injection` | Security (prompt injection) | Prompt injection is basically the same as a classic software bug like SQL injection, and gets fixed the same definitive way. | SQL injection has a fully reliable mechanical fix separating code from data; prompt injection's instruction-versus-content distinction is fuzzier since both are the same medium processed the same way, with no equally airtight fix. | Separating a form's data from its instructions is easy in two different languages; harder when both arrive as the same plain English sentence. | Ch. 28 |
| `only-obvious-inputs-cause-injection` | Security (prompt injection) | Only obviously suspicious-looking inputs can cause a prompt injection. | Injected instructions can be hidden in innocuous-looking text specifically to avoid detection, since there's no reliable built-in way to distinguish instruction-like content from ordinary content. | The malicious line in the executive's mail didn't look like a threat — that was exactly the point. | Ch. 28 |
| `alignment-alone-makes-safe` | Safety | Alignment training alone makes a system safe, regardless of what tools or agency it has. | Alignment shapes output tendencies but doesn't guarantee behavior under adversarial pressure; tool-using systems also need system-level safeguards outside the model. | A well-trained employee still works within spending approvals and access controls, even though they're trustworthy. | Ch. 28 |
| `ai-progress-guaranteed-pace` | AI's future trajectory | Because AI has improved so quickly and consistently, that trend is guaranteed to continue at the same pace indefinitely. | Scaling produces real but diminishing returns (Chapter 10); past improvement is evidence, not a guarantee of continued pace. | A marathon runner's steady first ten miles doesn't guarantee the same pace over the next ten once conditions change. | Ch. 29 |
| `every-announcement-is-new-architecture` | AI's future trajectory | Every new AI announcement represents a fundamentally new kind of intelligence or architecture. | Most announced improvements are specific, deliberately trained additions to the same core mechanism from Chapters 6 and 11, not a wholesale replacement of it. | A car with a new engine feature is still a car — recognizing genuinely new versus incrementally improved takes a specific, mechanism-level question. | Ch. 29 |
| `outdated-tools-mean-outdated-mechanisms` | AI's future trajectory | Since specific tools, benchmarks, and company names will become outdated, the technical mechanisms this book teaches will also become outdated. | Tokens, embeddings, attention, and gradient-based training are considerably more durable than any single product name or benchmark. | Specific car models come and go, but the underlying mechanics of an internal combustion engine explained the vehicles on the road for a century. | Ch. 29 |
| `finishing-book-means-knowing-everything` | Ongoing learning | Now that I've finished this book, I know everything I need to know about AI. | The book built a durable mental model, not an exhaustive or final inventory of every technique — new specific developments will keep appearing. | Learning to read fluently doesn't mean you've read every book ever written — it means every new book is now accessible. | Ch. 30 |
| `keeping-up-means-consuming-more` | Ongoing learning | Keeping up with AI means consuming as much news and commentary as possible, as fast as possible. | Depth on primary sources, filtered through Chapter 29's five questions, produces more real understanding than a high volume of unfiltered secondhand takes. | Reading one careful technical writeup closely teaches more than skimming twenty breathless headlines about the same announcement. | Ch. 30 |
| `non-technical-readers-cant-read-sources` | Ongoing learning | Because I'm not a programmer or ML researcher, I still can't meaningfully engage with primary technical sources like blog posts or papers. | This book taught tokens, embeddings, attention, training, retrieval, and agents without requiring math or programming at any point. | Learning to read sheet music doesn't require becoming a professional composer — it requires learning what the symbols mean. | Ch. 30 |
